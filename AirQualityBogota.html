About Bogotá's Air Quality Crisis

Aerial view of 100TH Street, one of the most transited streets in Bogotá D.C.
Centro histórico, La Candelaria

Bogotá is the capital of Colombia and one of the most important cities in Latin America. It boasts one of the highest industrial, financial, and commercial densities on the continent, in addition to its more than 8 million inhabitants. The city is located at a high altitude, approximately 2,625 meters above sea level, which contributes to the retention of particulate matter in the air. This, combined with critical mobility issues—such as heavy truck traffic and the BRT mass transit system—generates polluting emissions that further exacerbate the problem.

Key Pollutants Mapped

PM2.5 (Fine Particulate Matter) — particles smaller than 2.5 micrometres in diameter that penetrate deep into lung tissue and enter the bloodstream. Primary sources in Bogotá include diesel vehicles, construction sites and cooking fires in low-income peripheral neighbourhoods. Chronic exposure is strongly linked to respiratory and cardiovascular disease (WHO, 2021).

PM10 (Coarse Particulate Matter) — particles up to 10 micrometres, primarily generated by road dust, quarrying and industrial processes in the Fontibón and Kennedy localities, adjacent to El Dorado International Airport. Concentrations tend to peak during the dry seasons (January–March and July–August).

NO₂ (Nitrogen Dioxide) — a toxic gas produced mainly by transport vehicles such as TransMilenio BRT. For example, TransMilenio corridors show the highest Nitrogen Dioxide readings, consistent with global evidence linking traffic density to elevated nitrogen dioxide levels (Larsen et al., 2017).

Impact on People's Life

Air pollution in the city is one of the biggest health problems among the population. The increase in respiratory illnesses is closely linked to this situation, and socioeconomic segregation is also evident. The difference between social classes and sectors is even apparent in terms of air pollution levels in the city, as areas inhabited by people with higher incomes have lower levels. These areas also tend to have less traffic, virtually no industrial zones, more well-maintained green spaces, and less population density.

Using information extracted from the "District Secretariat of the Environment (SDA) operates the Bogotá Air Quality Monitoring Network (RMCAB)," an interactive real-time map is generated that, thanks to the open and freely accessible information provided by the Mayor's Office of Bogotá, allows us to identify patterns and propose solutions that can mitigate the impact on people's physical health, and even on their mental health, since better health and a lower perception of pollution help the visual enjoyment of the environment.

Methodology

Data Sources

The open-source data is obtained from OpenAQ, a platform that allows us to register information in real time; this information is therefore extracted from the RMCAB network (Secretaría Distrital de Ambiente). The information will be extracted from there in real time, making it easy to identify changes over time and across space.

Mapping Approach

The map distributed is based on the location of each monitoring station around the city. Marker size scales with the concentration reading register in our open-source. You can identify the three main pollutants by following the colour legend that defines the risk of each one.

Technologies Used

HTML5 · CSS3 (Grid, custom properties, animations) · JavaScript ES6+ · Leaflet.js 1.9.4 · Chart.js 4.4.1 · Google Fonts (Syne, DM Serif Display, Space Mono). No build tools, frameworks or website generators were used.
